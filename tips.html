The "What" and the "Why":
Crop rotation is the practice of planting different crops in a sequence on the same plot of land. This is done to optimize the level of nutrients in the soil and keep both pests and weeds away from the crops.
In Practice:
For a small garden, some sources recommend dividing the garden into quarters, picking a central spot, and picking where to plant by ‚Äúrotating‚Äù around that point. More details on which seeds to plant with others can be found in the Resources section below.

üíß Garden Setup

In-ground vs. raised bed:
Raised bed: Raised beds offer the main advantage of a longer growing season. Since the raised bed warms up the soil quicker in the spring, you can begin planting earlier. Raised beds also offer less soil compaction and therefore better drainage through the soil. Raised beds also typically have less necessary weeding and are considered lower maintenance than a traditional in-ground garden.
In-ground: Since you don‚Äôt need to physically build an in-ground garden, they are typically cheaper and quicker to get started. In-ground gardens also need to be watered less, as they do not dry out as quickly as a raised bed garden would. However, animals have easy access to your crops, so fencing might be necessary.
What you'll need:
Many raised garden bed kits are available in a multitude of sizes and materials. However, if you prefer to create your own, all you will need is some lumber with the correct size specifications for your bed, and some screws to hold it together. You will also need soil to add into your raised garden bed. It is important to do your research beforehand so that you know what type of soil your intended plants prefer.

Benefits:
Composting is a resourceful way to recycle the food scraps and yard trim you generate at home all year and manage your waste more sustainably.
How To:
Composting can be done in your backyard by having four key components: greens (fruit and veggie scraps, crushed eggshells, and grass clippings), browns (dry leaves, untreated wood chips, shredded brown bags), air, and water. You want to avoid glossy or colored paper, dairy or meat products, and grease.

Benefits:
Having house plants (or starting an indoor garden) in your space can improve mood, focus, and productivity. Indoor plants can also make you happier and even help you recover from illnesses! There are many ways to get these benefits inside your home beyond your typical house plant.
Hydroponics:
A great way to have a garden indoors (and maybe even cut down on some mess) is to use a hydroponic system. Hydroponics is a growing method that does not use soil and relies instead on nutrient-filled water to sustain the plants. The only plants that don‚Äôt do well in hydroponic systems are root crops (potatoes, onions, or beets) and tall or vining plants (trees or ivies). There are plenty of existing systems available for purchase, but there are also resources that teach you how to make your own system.
Windowsill Gardens:
Like the name suggests, a windowsill garden can either hang from your window or simply sit in it. They are a great option for people who have limited space, don‚Äôt have time to commit to an outdoor garden, or regularly have inclement weather conditions. Easy to grow items include: herbs, leafy greens, succulents, flowers, and many others.
